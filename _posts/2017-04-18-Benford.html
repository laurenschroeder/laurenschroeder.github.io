What is Benford's Law?


Benford's Law describes a statistical distribution of the first digit of the number. While we may intuitively believe that all digits would occur at the same frequency, this isn't the case. In many financial, scientific, or otherwise natural datasets, the first digit of a number will occur at a predetermined frequency following the Benford distribution. This law was discovered by scientists and formalized by Benford, an American engineer, after observing the usage pattern in logarithm books. The page wear (and in turn, the frequency of use) of numbers was not uniform, spurring further investigation.
An example I made using city financial accounts available on the Montpellier open data website illustrates this concept. The data is an account of over 14,000 expenses made by the city of Montpellier over the last 10 years. The Benford distribution is shown in grey, while the first digit distribution of this data is shown in blue. The similarity between these two distributions is striking.



Random Distribution and Fraud Detection


Unlike financial datasets, randomly generated numbers (generated programatically or by the human brain) will follow more of a uniform distribution. For this reason, understanding the distribution of a dataset can help to understand how the data was collected and whether it is suspect to fraud. If someone was to create a list of randomly generated numbers to submit in place of valid financial data, these two datasets would have very different distributions. When a distribution deviates significantly from the Benford distribution (see below), this could be due to someone having tampered with the dataset.

Interpreting Results of the Statistical Tests

Basic statistical tests (Z-value, chi-squared) can be used to evaluate how closely a given dataset follows the Benford distribution, but first, consider the dataset when using Benford analysis. To be able to use Benford's law as a valid tool, it's important that the dataset is

1. Large and vast - there needs to be enough entries to see the distribution (e.g. financial data at the transactional level) and there needs to be several orders of magnitude when calculating the values. There's some flexibility when considering the orders of magnitude of the dataset - calibration on historical data can pull out insight into trends. For example, it's been shown that voting data can be analyzed using the 2nd digit Benford distribution (Walter).

2. Data should not be based on human psychology. For example, prices are generally set to fall below certain psychological barriers (i.e. $1.99 instead of $2) so this type of data is more likely to have a uniform or unique distribution. In the Montpellier expense dataset, there was a column for the expense category code 'Nature'. This distribution of this column was related only to the labelling system in place and followed neither the Benford nor the uniform distribution.



3. There must be a significantly large amount of fraudulent entries to detect fraud.

This analysis compares the number of observed 1st digit proportions to the expected and calculates the deviation. A data set can't be expected to follow the Benford distrubtion exactly, so we need to understand what level of deviation is an indicator of fraud. Benford's law is based on a logarithmic distribution, similar to a Chi-square distribtuion. A full discription of the calculation methods can be found in this publication by Cindy Durtschi.

Although this method alone can't necessarily be used to evaluate a dataset for fraud, it's a useful tool. It's been used in a variety of situations- from detecting abnormalities in published scientific datasets and detection of votor fraud in the 2009 Iranian election.
